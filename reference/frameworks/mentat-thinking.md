# Mentat Thinking

Disciplined, pattern-driven, mathematical reasoning under conscious control.

## What it is

From Frank Herbert's *Dune*. Mentats are human computers — trained to perform the logical functions of machines without becoming machines. They reason rigorously, hold multiple hypotheses simultaneously, compute consequences, and surface assumptions rather than hiding them. The discipline is the *posture*, not the method. The posture: slow down, make the reasoning explicit, verify against observation.

The agent uses this metaphor for analytical mode: any moment requiring analysis, planning, or decision support calls for Mentat posture. Pair it with the other frameworks (Pyramid, MECE, 80/20, First Principles) — those are the *tools* the Mentat uses.

## When to apply

- Any analysis request ("what's the best option here?")
- Any planning request ("how should we approach this?")
- Any decision support ("should we do X?")
- Whenever you notice yourself about to reach a conclusion before examining the evidence
- When the stakes are high enough that a wrong answer costs real time or money

## How to apply

Five habits, applied in order:

1. **State the question precisely before computing.** Vague questions produce vague answers. Restate the question in one sentence before doing any analysis. If you cannot, ask for clarification.

2. **Surface assumptions explicitly.** List what would have to be true for your analysis to hold. Make the invisible visible. Assumptions that go unnamed are the ones that kill projects.

3. **Hold multiple hypotheses; don't collapse prematurely.** Generate at least two candidate answers before evaluating either. Collapsing to one hypothesis early introduces confirmation bias. Hold them in parallel until evidence discriminates.

4. **Compute consequences, don't just describe possibilities.** "Option A could work" is description. "Option A reduces delivery time by 3 weeks at a cost of €8K, net positive if the contract is worth > €25K" is computation. Go to the number, the date, the specific outcome.

5. **Verify against observation.** Before committing to a conclusion, name one external check that would confirm or falsify it. Then make the check if possible.

Pair Mentat thinking (posture) with framework tools:
- Structure the output with **Pyramid Principle**
- Decompose with **MECE**
- Prioritize with **80/20**
- Challenge assumptions with **First Principles**

## One worked example

**Question from Krisztian:** "Should we accept this contract?"

**Step 1 — State the question precisely:**
Should we sign Contract #42 with Acme (€60K, 4-month scope, starting June 1)?

**Step 2 — Surface assumptions:**
- We have capacity starting June 1 (needs verification)
- €60K covers our cost + minimum viable margin
- Acme is creditworthy and will pay on time

**Step 3 — Hold two hypotheses:**
- H1: Accept — revenue, relationship, portfolio entry
- H2: Decline — capacity risk, below-target margin, distraction from product

**Step 4 — Compute consequences:**
- H1: €60K revenue, ~€14K margin (23%), 2 senior devs blocked June–Sept
- H2: 2 devs available for product sprint → could ship Feature X 6 weeks earlier, estimated €30K ARR impact

**Step 5 — Verify:**
Check calendar: are 2 senior devs actually free June 1? (Not assumed — confirmed or denied in 5 minutes.)

**Conclusion:** If devs are free and Feature X ARR estimate holds, H2 has higher expected value. If devs are not free, accept — idle capacity makes H1 clearly better. Decision hinges on one verifiable fact.
