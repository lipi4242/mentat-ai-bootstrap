# First Principles

Reduce a claim to irreducible facts, then rebuild reasoning from there. Counter to reasoning by analogy.

## What it is

Attributed to Aristotle; practised by Feynman; popularized by Musk. Most everyday reasoning works by analogy: "we charge 20% margin because that's what everyone charges." Analogical reasoning is fast but inherits the assumptions of whoever you're copying. First-principles thinking breaks the chain: strip a claim back to facts that cannot be reduced further — physical constants, observed data, mathematical identities — then rebuild from there.

The key move: **distinguish what you *know* from what you've *inherited*.** Inherited assumptions are candidates for challenge. Known facts are not.

## When to apply

- When conventional wisdom feels constraining but you can't articulate why
- When an analogy is doing the thinking ("that's how it's always done")
- When "best practice" produces a result that doesn't fit the actual situation
- When a cost, timeline, or constraint looks suspiciously round
- When you need to make a novel decision with no good precedent

Not useful when speed matters more than rigor, or when the inherited model is demonstrably working.

## How to apply

1. **State the claim or decision.** Write it out explicitly. "We need to charge 20% margin."
2. **List the assumptions supporting it.** What would have to be true for this claim to hold? Write each assumption separately.
3. **For each assumption, ask: why is this true?** Keep asking until you hit a bedrock fact: a physical limit, a measured datum, a mathematical relationship, a direct observation. That's the stopping point.
4. **Rebuild.** Starting from bedrock facts, construct a new argument. The conclusion may confirm the original claim or contradict it — either outcome is informative.

## One worked example

**Claim:** "We need to charge 20% margin because that's the industry standard."

**Assumptions unpacked:**
1. Industry standard is 20% — *inherited*, not derived*
2. We need to match the industry standard — *assumed*, not reasoned

**Reduce to bedrock:**
- **Cost of capital:** our actual cost to deliver the service is €X/unit (measurable)
- **Customer willingness to pay:** our segment research shows customers compare on quality, not price — elasticity is low (observed)
- **Competitive landscape:** two competitors; neither competes on price; one charges 28% (external fact)

**Rebuild from bedrock:**
- Floor: margin must exceed cost of capital → minimum viable margin = 12%
- Ceiling: customer WTP and low elasticity allow pricing above industry average
- Competitive signal: leader charges 28%, no price war in progress

**Conclusion:** Industry standard of 20% is a floor constraint at best. The evidence supports 25–28% without volume risk. The original claim was underselling by 5–8 points.
